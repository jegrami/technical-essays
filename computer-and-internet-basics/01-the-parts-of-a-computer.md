## 2. Basic Anatomy of Your Computer: The Parts of a Computer

Every computer has the same basic parts inside it, whether it sits on a desk, folds shut like a laptop, or fits in your pocket as a phone. A phone is a computer too. It has the same core parts as a desktop, just built smaller and packaged differently. Once you know these parts, you know the shape of almost every device you use.

Start with the motherboard. The motherboard is the main circuit board inside the case. It holds sockets and slots for the other main parts, and it wires them all together. Think of it as the frame that the rest of the computer is built on. In a phone or tablet, the motherboard is often a single small board packed tightly behind the screen, but it does the same job.

The most important part plugged into the motherboard is the *processor*, a.k.a the CPU, short for central processing unit. The processor is the part that does the actual computing. Every calculation, every decision your computer makes, happens here. 

Inside the processor are three smaller parts that work together. One part, the arithmetic logic unit (ALU), does the math and comparisons. Another part, the control unit (CU), directs the flow of work and tells other parts what to do next. The third part comprises a small set of storage spots called registers that hold the tiny bits of data the processor is working with at that exact moment. We will not go deeper into how these parts cooperate here. For now, it is enough to know the processor is the brain of the computer.

A processor's speed is often measured in gigahertz (GHz). One GHz means one billion basic operations per second. Most modern computers and phones run somewhere between two and five GHz, which means they are capable of performing two to five billion basic instructions every second, just to keep up with you tapping a screen or typing a sentence.

A processor is also often described by how many *cores* it has. A core is a complete processing unit on its own, capable of carrying out instructions by itself. It's the actual thing on the CPU that does the work. Modern processors usually have several cores built into a single chip, so it is almost like having several small processors working side by side instead of just one. We will look at how cores let your computer do several things at once in a later article.

The processor needs somewhere to hold the data and instructions it is actively using. That place is memory, usually called RAM (random access memory). RAM holds whatever your computer is working on right now,such as the app you have open, the document you are editing, the webpage you are reading. RAM is fast, but it is temporary. When you turn off your computer, everything in RAM disappears. That is why you save a document before you shut down. 

Not all memory disappears when the power goes off. A small amount of memory called ROM (read only memory), holds a fixed set of instructions that do not change during normal use. This is where your computer keeps the startup instructions it needs before it can even load the rest of the operating system. We will walk through exactly how this works in the next article, on what happens when you turn on your computer.

RAM and ROM only hold data while your computer is actively using it or checking basic startup instructions. To keep your files, photos, and apps around permanently, your computer needs a different kind of storage. This is called *secondary storage*, or just storage. Older computers, and many desktops today, use a hard disk drive (hdd), which stores data magnetically on spinning disks. Most modern laptops and phones use a solid state drive instead(ssd). SSDs are the modern, improved version of HDD. They have no moving parts, so they are faster and more durable. Many devices also connect to cloud storage.  With a cloud storage, your data lives on someone else's storage, on a computer far away (often called a *server*), and travels to you over the internet whenever you need it. 

So far we have talked about parts that mostly sit inside the case: the motherboard, the processor, RAM, ROM, and storage. These parts need a way to talk to each other, and that is the job of the *bus*. The bus is a set of electrical pathways on the motherboard that carries data between parts. When you open a file, the processor does not reach out and grab the data itself. Instead, it sends a request over the bus to your storage. A short while later, storage sends the data back over the same bus, and the processor picks it up from memory. Every part inside your computer, and many parts connected from outside, such as external drives or a printer plugged into a USB port, all pass their data through some version of a bus. You have likely seen the term "USB" on a cable or port. USB (universal serial bus) is one common example of a bus, built to connect devices from outside the case rather than the ones soldered inside it.

Displaying anything on screen also takes real work, more than the main processor can comfortably do on its own while handling everything else. So that job is handled by a dedicated component called the GPU (graphics processing unit). A GPU is built to perform the massive number of calculations needed to render the pixels that make up everything you see on your screen. This includes text, images, videos, animations, and even the mouse cursor. On many laptops and phones, the GPU is built into the same chip as the processor. On many desktops, it sits on its own separate card, plugged into the motherboard.

None of these inside parts are useful unless you can get information in and get results out. Input devices are how you send data and instructions into the computer. The keyboard sends the letters and commands you type. The *mouse*—or, on a *phone*/*tablet*, your finger on the touchscreen—tells the computer where you are pointing and what you are selecting. A *microphone* captures sound, while a *webcam* or *camera* captures images and video. On many modern devices, the camera can also capture documents and turn them into digital files. Each of these devices has its own way of alerting the processor that new input has arrived, which we will cover in the article on input devices.

Output devices show you the results of everything the computer has done. The *screen* displays text, images, and video, drawn with help from the GPU. *Speakers* turn digital audio back into sound you can hear. A *printer* produces a paper copy of a document. A *projector* throws the screen's image onto a wall or a large surface for a room full of people to see.

That covers the main parts of a computer and how they connect to each other: the motherboard as the frame, the processor as the brain, RAM and ROM as memory, storage for anything permanent, the bus carrying data between all of it, and input and output devices as the way you and the computer exchange information.
