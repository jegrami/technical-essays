
# You Have Never Really Touched Anyone in Your Life

It feels obvious that you are touching something right now. Your fingers press against a keyboard. Your back rests against a chair. If someone sits beside you, your shoulders meet.

It feels solid.

But at the level of physics (or rather, *at the level of atoms*), contact never quite happens.

Everything around you is made of atoms. An atom has a tiny nucleus at its center and a cloud of electrons surrounding it. Almost all of the atom’s mass sits in that nucleus. Almost all of its volume is empty space.

To understand why it's impossible to "touch" anything, you need to know the basic structure of atoms and how electrons behave. 

If you magnify an atom until it's as big as a 100-meter football field, the nucleus would be only as big as a grain of sand sitting in the middle of the field. Yet that nucleus, whose size would be like a grain of sand if the whole atom were as big as a standard football pitch, accounts for 99.95 percent the entire weight of the atom. The remaining 0.05 percent weight belongs to the electrons buzzing around the edge of the field. Everything else&mdash;the entire field between&mdash;is empty space. That's  why sentients say an atom is so small that it's mostly empty space, mostly nothing. 
 
And you are made of atoms. The chair you are sitting on is made of atoms. The keyboard beneath your fingers is made of atoms. Heck&mdash;everything  around you, even the ground you walk on, is made of atoms.

To understand why you can't really touch anything, you need to understand the behavior of electrons.

Electrons carry negative charge. Charges of the same sign (`+` vs `+` or `-` vs `-`) repel, just as two magnets of the same pole (`north` vs `north` or `south` vs `south`) push each other away. This is not a mild effect. The electric force between electrons is one of the strongest forces in physics.

Thinking of contact at the atomic level, when you move your finger toward a wall, what is actually happening is your atoms moving toward the atoms of the wall. So what approaches first are not solid surfaces but electron clouds, the outside edges of atoms. The negatively charged electrons in your skin draw near to the negatively charged electrons in the wall. As the distance shrinks, the repulsive force between them rises sharply. The closer they get, the stronger the resistance. 

Before atoms can overlap in any classical sense, that force pushes back.

What you experience as “touch” is your nervous system detecting this resistance.

Your skin contains receptors that respond to pressure and deformation. When electromagnetic repulsion prevents further compression, those receptors fire. Signals travel to your brain. The brain interprets the pattern as solidity, texture, warmth, or softness.

You do not feel atoms meeting. You feel forces resisting.

There is another principle at work: the Pauli Exclusion Principle. Electrons belong to a class of particles called fermions. Fermions are particles that cannot share the same quantum state. In simple terms, no two of them can exist in exactly the same place in exactly the same way at the same time. When atoms are pushed very close together, their electrons cannot simply merge into identical states. Quantum mechanics forbids it.

This restriction contributes to the rigidity of matter. It is one reason matter maintains structure instead of collapsing inward. Electrostatic repulsion provides the push; quantum exclusion prevents overlap at the deepest level, like your hand passing through a class of water as you try to grab it, since , you know, you and the glass are made of atoms and atoms are mostly empty space.

So “touch” is not the meeting of solid surfaces. It is the combined effect of electromagnetic repulsion and quantum law. These prevent atoms from merging into one another. There is always a gap between two objects touching. The separation is tiny&mdash;fractions of a nanometer&mdash;but it's always there.

If atomic nuclei were forced into direct union, it would require extreme energies, like those inside stars. Everyday contact never approaches that condition. Instead, surfaces settle into a balance of forces. Very close. Never fused.

This does not mean touch is unreal. It means it is different from what we imagine.

There are no tiny billiard balls pressing together. There are fields interacting. There are charges repelling. There are quantum rules enforcing boundaries.

When you hold someone’s hand, your atoms do not interpenetrate theirs. Electron clouds approach. Forces rise. Your nerves register pressure. Your brain constructs warmth and presence from that signal.

At the most intimate scale, there remains a narrow distance that cannot be crossed.

We call the result touch.
