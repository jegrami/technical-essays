
**What Happened to the web?**

Before social media, people built their own spaces on the World Wide Web. They made websites to share their favorite things and connect with others on their own terms, in their own way, without conforming to the agenda or policy of any platform. That freedom is now gone.

Back then we didn't reels or timelines or feeds or social media. We had homepages. And when you visited a page, you found links to other interesting pages. You could spend all day moving from one homepage to another, discovering new places and information as you went. 

Netscape was the first attempt at a web browser, long before Edge or FireFox or  Chrome. It had a discovery feature called "what's cool" that curated interesting, innovative, fun, weird, or noteworthy new homepages that had appeared on the web. 

Assuming you knew how to write a few html tags, you could  build your own homepage in a few hours. There were no databases to install and setup, no frameworks or plugins, UI components, and certainly no such thing as cookies. ##

Every page was coded  by hand, in unadulterated html. And if there were many pages, the navigation menu was built by hand. There were no content management systems. We had table of content instead, also managed by hand. A well-managed, well-organized homepage was a sign of pride. People "tended" to their homepages like a personal garden. And indeed it was. 

Homepages were quirky and highly personal, full of interesting or useful stuff about a topic or a person.
People built pages for all kinds of things. For work, for showcasing pictures of their pets, for expressing their hatred of Internet Explorer. Someone had a website that was just pictures of Vietnam. The crazy thing is that however niche your site was, you would always find people who liked the same damn thing.

Today, the internet is full of websites that are dead ends. No links to other places, no trail to follow. The idea of "surfing the web" — where you start on one website, find links to others, click through, and discover something new — is practically dead. Killed by social media and the corporate internet. Replaced by infinite scrolling and algorithm-driven platforms that decide what you see and when.

And there was artistry on the internet once. People designed their personal sites however they liked. Now we're handed the same template as everyone else, designed and imposed by corporations. Sure you could change your profile picture and maybe your cover photo and username. But that's about as much customization you get.

Do you know how large the internet is? Have you considered how much of it you've never seen because you spend all your time on the same three social media sites?

Social media removed the need — or so it seemed — for building and maintaining personal sites. And the internet has been less fun ever since. The modern web encourages us to stay in our lanes and scroll our feeds, never wander. 

This is a call for the revival of the indie web. Build your own site. All you need is html. Make it feel truly personal. Reclaim your own little corner of the internet, then tend it the way you would a garden. Own your space. Own your data. Own your piece of the web ... before there's nothing left worth owning.
